Autonomous Wheelchair

×

Problem Statement


How can we make it easier for people with motor and visual impairments to travel independantly, without the help of a caretaker?

Problem Description


For blind people, it is often hard to get around without help. Traditionally, white canes are used by visually impaired wheelchair riders, who scan the area around the chair while it is moving. However, the caneâ€™s detection range is limited by its length, which means that riders are often unaware of obstacles in their path until it's too late.

Project Details


Duration:
July 2013 - April 2014

Role:
User Researcher, Prototyping Engineer

Key Takeaways


  • Access to a different type of users (persons with low vision or blindness) is crucial for a product aimed at these populations.
  • I am not the user; Different people have different needs, and it is important to understand these needs before diving into design and development.

Proposed Solution


We propose a wheelchair system which calculates the optimal path from point A to B and reroutes upon detecting obstacles in its path. The wheelchair has a Braille number keypad as input method to indicate the destination that the wheelchair must travel to. Sensors are used to detect obstacles and a rerouting logic determines the next feasible path.

Process


We began by interviewing blind persons and their caretakers to learn how they currently navigate in their home or office environment and the challenges they face. We then prototyped a wheelchair system using a line-following algorithm and integrated obstacle detection sensors.

User Interviews

We conducted 4 interviews with two target users, one caretaker and one doctor. Interviews revealed problems associated with costs and availability of power wheelchairs which with advanced features like obstacle detection and dynamic rerouting.

 Most patients can't afford the autonomous navigation technology in the market. 
 I get by most days by asking a family member to help me out. 
 I am not comfortable with letting a stranger look after my father, but I can't be with him all the time. 

Workflow Design

From our interview data, we decided to narrow down to three key features - automatic navigation, obstacle detection and obstacle avoidance. We started off by designing the algorithm that the product would use for obstacle detection and rerouting. When user inputs a new destination, the current location would be marked as the start node, and the wheelchair would progressively move to the next node, provided no obstacles are detected. On encountering an obstacle, it would go back to previous node and compute the new path. At each node, the wheelchair checks if the current node is the destination.

Product Prototyping

The working environment of the prototype was set up such that it would follow a fixed grid based map with significant destinations located at grid points. Line-following logic was implemented to ensure that the robot did not deviate from its path and followed the grid map to reach the destination.

The prototype was mounted with infrared sensors for obstacle detection. Rerouting logic was implemented so that upon detecting an obstacle, the robot would backtrack to the previous gridpoint and recalculate the most efficient path to destination.

A braille keypad was used as input which would allow users to enter their desired destination. Numbers on the keypad were mapped to specific destinations in the robot's working environment.

Outcome


The 10 - month long project culminated in a working prototype which was presented to an audience of peers and instructors. This included a demonstration of the obstacle detection and re-routing capabilities of the prototype as well as the mapping of real locations to a numeric input.
